Experimental Details

CLTR Experimental Design

The Chrono-Light Thermal Response study is structured as a controlled human-environment experiment that aligns light exposure, time-of-day, and thermal perturbation within a single protocol. It is built as a repeated-measures laboratory study designed to support both DC1 and DC2 through synchronized perception, physiology, behavior, and environmental observation.

Design

A 2 × 2 repeated-measures structure crossing bright versus dim light with morning versus midday exposure windows.

Cohort

The study includes 20 healthy adults, balanced by sex, each completing four sessions, one in each study condition.

Protocol

Each session lasts about 185 minutes and follows a structured sequence of acclimation, repeated fan cooling, rewarming, fan-free control, and steady-state observation.

Exposure Logic

Participants were studied in a warm indoor chamber at approximately 30 °C under two illuminance regimes and two diurnal time windows. Bright and dim light conditions were implemented in separate sessions, while morning and midday timing allowed the project to examine chrono-physiological structure alongside thermal response.

Measurement Scope

The CLTR dataset combines indoor environmental measurements, repeated questionnaire observations, fan-use behavior, wearable sensing, and laboratory-grade physiology. This multimodal structure is central to the project because it enables joint analysis of thermal perception, physiological adaptation, and participant-driven behavioral regulation.

DC1 Perspective

DC1 primarily led the computational and data-integration side of CLTR, including curation of the multimodal dataset, framework development, data orchestration, statistical modeling, and machine learning model development. This work established the analytical infrastructure required to organize, process, interpret, and reuse CLTR data as a research-grade computational resource.

DC2 Perspective

DC2 primarily led the experimental and methodological side of CLTR, including development of the exposure protocol, execution of the study sessions, data collection, methodological design, and the scientific framing of the first paper. This work defined the empirical basis of the project and ensured that the dataset and subsequent analyses remained grounded in a rigorous experimental architecture.
